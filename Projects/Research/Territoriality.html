Territoriality in the Digital and Urban Landscape

Sack conceives of territoriality as a spatial strategy that individuals, groups, and organizations use to achieve particular social and political ends. Sack (p.19) defines territoriality as “the attempt by an individual or group to affect, influence, or control people, phenomena, and relationships, by delimiting and asserting control over a geographic area. This area will be called the territory.” For Sack (p. 2), “Territoriality is intimately related to how people use the land, how they organize themselves in space, and how they give meaning to place.” He further asserts: “Territoriality... is a historically sensitive use of space, especially since it is socially constructed and depends on who is controlling whom and why [we may add: ‘and when’]. It is the key geographical component in understanding how society and space are interconnected” (p. 3). Thus: “Territoriality, then, forms the backcloth of human spatial relations and conceptions of space... Human spatial relations are the results of influence and power. The territory is the primary spatial form power takes” (p. 26).

Reflections on Islamabad

Islamabad, as a planned city, demonstrates the physical manifestation of territoriality through its design and governance. The capital's layout reflects a spatial strategy aimed at asserting state control, shaping social interactions, and influencing urban life. The city’s zoning system—a hallmark of its urbanism—defines residential, commercial, diplomatic, and green areas, reinforcing the concept of territories as instruments of control and order. These zones reflect a negotiated relationship between state power and the lived experiences of its residents, where territorial boundaries are reified through planning decisions and social practices.

Sack’s conception of territoriality can also be expanded to understand the evolving digital landscape within Islamabad. With the advent of digital technologies, new layers of territoriality emerge, blending the physical with the virtual. Digital platforms like Facebook and Google introduce a form of "deterritorialization," where geographic boundaries become fluid. However, they simultaneously create “reterritorialized” digital spaces that mirror and influence urban life.

For instance, in Islamabad, platforms like OLX and Daraz have transformed traditional marketplaces into digital territories. These platforms collect and process user data to curate localized experiences, influencing how goods and services are exchanged. Similarly, social media platforms serve as tools for political discourse and social mobilization, redefining territorial control by creating virtual spaces for civic engagement. The use of Twitter for raising awareness about urban issues, such as environmental degradation in the Margalla Hills or traffic concerns, reflects the negotiation of territoriality within a digital framework.

As Sack suggests, territoriality is not merely about physical boundaries but about power and control. In the context of Islamabad, the digital realm amplifies these dynamics. Facebook and Google, for instance, create territories by capturing user behaviors and patterns, akin to Sack’s “empty-able space.” This data is capitalized upon to generate insights, influencing not only the virtual experience but also urban governance. For example, geolocation data from Google Maps contributes to urban planning decisions, such as optimizing traffic flows or identifying high-demand commercial zones.

The interplay between urbanism and digital platforms in Islamabad reveals a dual territoriality: one rooted in physical urban design and the other in virtual spaces. The latter is increasingly significant, as digital platforms impose boundaries through text limits, image sizes, and algorithms while allowing users to negotiate their usage. Instagram’s conventions of liking, posting, and following create symbolic territories that foster new forms of interaction and community within the city’s digital populace.

This negotiation is evident in how digital tools are appropriated for diverse purposes. In Islamabad, Twitter has become a platform for political activism, Instagram a tool for showcasing the city’s evolving cultural identity, and Facebook a marketplace for local entrepreneurs. These platforms provide structured spaces for interaction but are continually redefined by users’ behaviors and practices, reflecting the city’s dynamic interplay between the digital and the physical.

In conclusion, Islamabad's urbanism and sense of territoriality are increasingly shaped by the integration of digital technologies. The city’s planned zones and digital platforms represent interconnected forms of territorial control, where both physical spaces and virtual environments negotiate power, identity, and interaction. This dual territoriality underscores the evolving nature of how people organize themselves in space and give meaning to place in the 21st century.
